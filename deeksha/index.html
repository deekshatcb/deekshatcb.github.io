About

I am a PhD graduate from IISER Mohali, trained as computational structural biologist in the lab of Dr. Shashi B. Pandit. With a passion for analyzing data and uncovering the hidden stories within, I enjoy making sense of complex datasets and revealing the insights buried beneath the surface.

       I’m fascinated by biological processes, where every component interacts intricately to regulate the processes of life. The complexity and robustness of biological systems, with multiple layers of control, never cease to amaze me. As scientists, we not only delve deep to understand these intricacies but also draw inspiration from nature’s grand orchestration to create something meaningful. My PhD research focuses on one of nature’s molecular machines,"enzymes". These catalysts drive the molecular transformations that are essential for life, acting as the alchemists behind the biochemical processes that sustain living organisms.

       While my PhD research explored the precision of enzymes at the molecular level, my current work expands this perspective to the broader evolutionary dialogue between host and pathogen proteins. Building on this foundation, I joined ABopen Lab as a Research Associate, where I investigate the evolutionary interplay between host mitochondrial proteins and pathogen proteins. This work explores how pathogens, with their greater evolutionary freedom, develop strategies to exploit the host’s constrained immune responses. By targeting interactions where the host cannot easily adapt, pathogens secure an advantage in the constant arms race for survival.

Research Experience

Understanding the structurally discerning features of the active site of substrate promiscuous and specialist enzymes.

2019-2020      Publication
Substrate promiscuity Enzyme structure Active site Structural features

Enzyme promiscuity is the ability of (some) enzymes to perform alternate reactions or catalyze non-cognate substrate(s). Previous studies on some enzymes have suggested that flexibility, hydrophobicity, and active site protonation state could play an important role in enzyme promiscuity. However, it is not known yet whether substrate promiscuous enzymes have distinctive structural characteristics compared to specialist enzymes, which are specific for a substrate. In pursuit to address this, we have systematically compared substrate/catalytic binding site structural features of substrate promiscuous with those of specialist enzymes.

Study Highlights:

  • To examine whether substrate promiscuous enzymes have distinguishing structural features structural attributes of binding/catalytic sites are compared between the curated dataset of promiscuous and specialist enzymes (having no known alternate substrate(s))
  • Construction of curated datasets of promiscuous and specialist enzymes.
  • Surprisingly, substrate promiscuous and specialist enzymes have similar binding/catalytic site structural attributes.
  • The substrate preference of enzymes (promiscuity) could be defined as a continuum feature that varies from narrow to broad range of substrates.

Recent studies have also alluded that promiscuity is widespread among enzymes. Based on these observations, we propose that substrate promiscuity could be defined as a continuum feature that varies from narrow (specialist) to broad range of substrate preferences.

Exon nomenclature and classification of transcripts database (ENACTdb): a resource for analyzing alternative splicing mediated proteome diversity

2019 - 2022      Publication Database      Publication Method
Alternative Splicing Exon-nomenclature Transcript Diversity Proteoform visualization Genomic annotation

Eukaryotic genomes compensate for disparities between gene count and expressed protein by several mechanisms, where the contribution of alternate splicing (AS) is commendable. To understand how splicing impacts gene products, a distinction between alternate transcription (ATR) and alternate translation (ATL) and focus on AS is necessary. I developed the ENACT framework to address this need, which systematically annotates and classifies exons from a gene perspective.

Study Highlights:

  • ENACT annotations were performed on protein-coding genes of nematodes, flies, fish, mice, and humans, with the results presented in the Nightingale library views available as ENACTdb at https://www.iscbglab.in/enactdb.
  • Identified diverse exon features, including variable prevalence in transcripts, differential translational characteristics, and splice site preferences, with a noteworthy trade-off between exon skipping events and alternate splice site choices across organisms.
  • A significant abundance of alternate exons in untranslated regions (UTRs) and intervening regions between UTRs and coding sequences (CDS) was noticed in mammals, underscoring their regulatory roles.
  • The synergistic interplay between ATR and AS processes shapes the diversity of the human proteome, where AS-impacted region exhibits a higher fraction of domains per gene with this fraction remaining relatively stable across isoforms.

Deciphering binding site conformational variability of substrate promiscuous and specialist enzymes

2020-2022      Publication
Conformational variability Molecular Dynamics Enzyme-Ligand Simulations tICA Enzyme promiscuity Conformational clustering Enzyme-ligand docking Functional variability score (fχ)

Promiscuous activities expand enzymes’ functional landscape, enabling evolution to catalyze novel biochemical reactions or rational design of enzymes. The molecular basis of substrate promiscuity remains elusive. To explore the role of active site conformational flexibility in substrate promiscuity, we compared the conformational binding states in substrate promiscuous and specialist enzymes by analyzing long-term explicit water molecular dynamics.

Study Highlights:

  • We performed a quantitative estimation of the variability using the native functionality score, which quantifies the similarity of conformations to the native binding structure.
  • Presented a case study of four enzymes, we observed multiple or restrictive substrate binding competent conformers in both enzyme groups
  • These case studies demonstrated that varying degrees of binding site conformational variability, as assessed visually or quantitatively using native functionality score, are observed in both categories of enzymes

This argues in favour of substrate promiscuity being an inherent feature, and its extent could be an enzyme-specific property dependent on its biological role in the cell. Understanding the conditions under which latent activities can be observed is crucial for fully realizing substrate promiscuity, emphasizing that it is a nuanced and enzyme-specific property.

Other Projects

  • Analyzing network community structures derived from residue correlations among respective promiscuous and specialist enzymes
    • 2022-2023

    Having studied substrate promiscuity at structural and conformational attributes of active site, we analysed the molecular dynamics of 10 enzymes (5 each from promiscuous and specialist category) to evaluate deciphering signals through residue correlation networks and frustrated interactions in the folded structures of enzymes.

  • Studying the effect of genetic perturbations on growth of E. coli using genome scale metabolic models.
    • 2017-2018

    E. coli metabolic reconstruction iJO1366 was implemented and model simulations suggested that a Δicl (isocitrate lyase) mutant knockout would grow normally in oleate M9 medium. However, experimental results showed defective growth, consistent with prior studies highlighting ICL’s importance during fatty acid metabolism. Despite this, in silico simulations indicated normal growth, suggesting flux rerouting compensating for the ICL deletion. Analysis revealed flux diversion towards ICDHyr instead of ICL, with unexpected pyruvate synthesis from Acetyl CoA. Fixing this reaction’s directionality redirected fluxes through ICL, confirming its essential role in the absence of alternative pathways, as validated by null phenotype upon ICL deletion.

  • Experiments to examine the growth of E. coli while changing the carbon source.
    • 2018

    Conducted experiments demonstrated that transitioning the carbon source from glucose to galactonate induces a diauxic growth curve when cultivated in minimal media. This observation underscores the necessity of a diauxic period for the production of enzymes and other cellular machinery crucial for metabolizing galactonate as the primary carbon source.
